# It starts with a stroller at a fairy-tale lake

There are places where time seems to stand still. Places where imagination
and reality blur into each other and, for a moment, the everyday noise just
fades out. Mine is in Brühl, Germany.

My parents used to push me around the fairy-tale lake at
[Phantasialand](ref:phantasialand?bare) in a stroller — past more than 40
fairy-tale scenes full of lovingly built sets and moving animatronics.
Little Red Riding Hood, Cinderella, Ali Baba. For me that was never just a
day at a park. It was stepping into living stories, staged with a level of
detail that made you half believe the figures might wake up any second.

And then there was the **"1001 Nights"** gondola ride — in service until
2009 and the first themed dark ride of its kind in Germany. In the late
80s I glided through scenes of Sinbad, Ali Baba and Scheherazade with my
dad, from the massive dragon's maw to the mysterious dripstone cave. My
father passed away in 2019. To this day, those rides are like little
lanterns of happiness for me: they keep my childhood warm — and my memory
of him right along with it.

I think that's what theme parks can do when they're built with real love:
they store moments that last a lifetime. A new high score in
[Maus au Chocolat](ref:phantasialand/maus-au-chocolat?bare) still puts a
childish grin on my face — even though my partner keeps sniping the
5,000-point mice right in front of me and robbing me of the record.
Pancakes and shakes at **Phenie's** in Wuze Town afterwards, and the day
is perfect. There's a reason this park gets to call itself "Europe's Most
Immersive Theme Park."

## The other half of the story

In parallel there was always a second me: the developer. More than 25
years of software by now — many of them in the VPN world, first as Head of
Software Development at **CyberGhost**, today as a Distinguished Software
Engineer at **ExpressVPN**. Systems that millions of people use at the
same time without ever having to think about them. Plus open source,
smart-home adapters and side projects — the stuff that lives on
[arns.dev](https://arns.dev) and [GitHub](https://github.com/PArns).

Two passions that ran side by side for decades — until that one moment
every park fan knows: sixty minutes in the
[Taron](ref:phantasialand/taron?bare) standby, the display lying "around
45 min" to your face, while two stations over a half-empty family ride
sends car after car into the void. And there I stood: someone who
processes real-time data streams for a living — with absolutely no idea
whether this particular queue was worth it.

> "Let me just track some wait times." — _three years and half a million
> lines of code later…_

What grew out of it is [park.fan](/): a **real-time nervous system for
theme parks**. Live waits from over **150 parks** and **5,000+
attractions**, updated by the minute. Our own AI model that sees crowds
coming **up to 365 days** out — holidays, weather and schedules already
factored in. Free, no ads, no corporation looking over our shoulder. So
that your time in a park goes where it belongs: magic, memories — and
high-score rematches.

## So why a blog on top?

Because a wait time without context is just a rumour with a decimal point.
"50 minutes" — the same number three times, three completely different
stories:

- **50 minutes** for a **flat ride**? Pure waste. Three real coasters would
  have fit in that window.
- **50 minutes** for the
  [Velocicoaster](ref:universal-islands-of-adventure/jurassic-world-velocicoaster?bare)
  on a Saturday afternoon? Strong — the peak is usually around 85, so you
  basically caught the express lane.
- **20 minutes** for
  [Voltron Nevera](ref:europa-park/voltron-nevera-powered-by-rimac?bare) a
  year after opening? Dream day — or the crowds have finally spread out.

The number's in the chart. From now on, the story behind it lives here.

**Trip reports.** Long, opinionated, with photos — and with real data from
the exact park that was actually visited. Not "it was nice," but:
"[Magic Kingdom](ref:magic-kingdom-park?bare), May 15th, 09:08 rope drop —
here was the plan, here's what worked, here's what flopped."

**Data deep-dives.** What does a park's wait-time history _really_ say?
When is [Taron](ref:phantasialand/taron?bare) actually worth it? And do
our forecasts hold up? (The hit rate is out in the open on the
[How-to page](/howto) — no cheating.)

**News.** Short, honest, no SEO sermon. When something happens, it's here —
faster than you can dismiss a cookie banner.

And the best part: the posts are **alive**. Charts, maps and wait times
pull straight from park.fan — what you read is what the park is doing
right now. The two main characters of this story, live and unretouched:

## One more thing

This blog is and stays **not a marketing flyer**. park.fan is free, has no
ads, belongs to no one but curiosity. If a park is run badly, we'll write
about it. If an app is worse than the official one, we'll say so too. Got a
topic itching you? Reach me — the email's in the [imprint](/impressum).
