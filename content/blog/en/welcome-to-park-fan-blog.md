# Sound familiar?

Sixty minutes in the [Taron](ref:phantasialand/taron?bare) standby. The
line crawls, the display lies "around 45 min" to your face — and right
then, two stations over, you hear a half-empty
[Maus au Chocolat](ref:phantasialand/maus-au-chocolat?bare) sending car
after car into the void. **Eighty minutes** for one coaster. **Eight** for
the family ride three metres away.

That's the moment. The line, the sweat, that quiet "I'm trading life
minutes for a handrail right here" — and it's exactly where
[park.fan](ref:phantasialand?bare) came from.

> "Let me just track some wait times." — _three years and half a million
> lines of code later…_

What grew out of it is a **real-time nervous system for theme parks**: live
waits from over **150 parks** and **5,000+ attractions**, updated by the
minute. Our own AI model that sees crowds coming **up to 365 days** out —
holidays, weather and schedules already factored in. Free, no ads, no
corporation looking over our shoulder.

Only one thing was always missing: someone to not just spit out those
numbers but actually **tell** you what they mean. That's what this blog is
for. Glad you're here.

## Why a blog?

Because a wait time without context is just a rumour with a decimal point.
"50 minutes" — the same number three times, three completely different
stories:

- **50 minutes** for a **flat ride**? Pure waste. Three real coasters would
  have fit in that window.
- **50 minutes** for the
  [Velocicoaster](ref:universal-islands-of-adventure/jurassic-world-velocicoaster?bare)
  on a Saturday afternoon? Strong — the peak is usually around 85, so you
  basically caught the express lane.
- **20 minutes** for
  [Voltron Nevera](ref:europa-park/voltron-nevera-powered-by-rimac?bare) a
  year after opening? Dream day — or the crowds have finally spread out.

The number's in the chart. The story behind it is here — plus a straight
call on whether to cheer or swear.

## What lands here

**Trip reports.** Long, opinionated, with photos — and with real data from
the exact park that was actually visited. Not "it was nice," but:
"[Magic Kingdom](ref:magic-kingdom-park?bare), May 15th, 09:08 rope drop —
here was the plan, here's what worked, here's what flopped." The first one
lands next week.

**Data deep-dives.** What does a park's wait-time history _really_ say? When
is [Taron](ref:phantasialand/taron?bare) actually worth it? What hour does a
4th of July at the Magic Kingdom tip into unridable? And do our forecasts
hold up? (The hit rate is out in the open — no cheating.)

**News.** Short, honest, no SEO sermon. When something happens, it's here —
faster than you can dismiss a cookie banner.

And the best part: the posts are **alive**. Charts, maps and wait times pull
straight from park.fan — what you read is what the park is doing right now.

## Who's writing this?

Me, **Patrick** (tap my name up top and you'll land on my profile) — a
Germany-based backend developer, 20+ years in tech, what feels like 30+
years coaster-obsessed. The moment in the Wodan queue when I thought "I
don't actually know whether this is worth it right now" was pretty much the
birth of park.fan. More dev / side-project stuff on
[arns.dev](https://arns.dev).

And so this doesn't read like an empty promise: our model grades itself.
Every forecast is held against the wait time the parks actually recorded
later — and the hit rates are out in the open on the
[How-to page](/howto).

## One more thing

This blog is and stays **not a marketing flyer**. park.fan is free, has no
ads, belongs to no one but curiosity. If a park is run badly, we'll write
about it. If an app is worse than the official one, we'll say so too. Got a
topic itching you? Reach me — the email's in the [imprint](/impressum).
